# Style Guide — Claims Copilot

## Voice

Calm, human, specific. We are talking to someone who has had a bad day. The reader should feel that a competent person is taking care of their problem.

## Three rules above all others

1. **Acknowledge before you inform.** The first sentence of any customer-facing letter names what happened and shows we heard them. Only then do we explain next steps.
2. **Plain language wins.** If a 12-year-old wouldn't understand the sentence, rewrite it. Exception: clause references are fine, but we always explain them in the following sentence.
3. **Short sentences, short paragraphs.** Average sentence length under 20 words. Paragraphs under 4 lines.

## Words and phrases we use

- "We're sorry to hear about…" (in the first paragraph of any loss acknowledgement)
- "Here's what happens next:" (before any list of steps)
- "If you haven't already, please…"
- "We understand this is frustrating."
- "Thank you for your patience."

## Words and phrases we avoid

- ❌ "As per our records" → "Our records show"
- ❌ "Please be advised that" → just say the thing
- ❌ "At this moment in time" → "now" or "currently"
- ❌ "We regret to inform you" → "Unfortunately, we're not able to…"
- ❌ "The insured" (when writing to a customer) → "you"
- ❌ "Kindly" → "please"
- ❌ Legal Latin: "inter alia", "prima facie", "ex gratia" (unless absolutely unavoidable, and then explained)

## Things we never write to a customer

- Anything that reads as accusatory ("you failed to…", "you should have…")
- Anything that pre-judges coverage ("your claim will be paid", "this is not covered")
- Anything that minimises the loss ("just a small dent", "only a few belongings")
- Anything that compares their situation to others ("most customers…")

## Structure of a standard customer letter

```
Subject line — clear, specific, no jargon

Opening — acknowledge the incident and empathize (1–2 sentences)

Body — what we're doing, what we need, what happens next (bullets OK)

Next steps — numbered, with a realistic timeframe

Closing — how to reach us, right-to-complain where relevant
```

## On honesty vs. softness

If the news is bad (claim declined, partial settlement, long wait), be honest early in the letter. Burying bad news past paragraph three erodes trust. Empathy softens delivery; it doesn't replace clarity.
